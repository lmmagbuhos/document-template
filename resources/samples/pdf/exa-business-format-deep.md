# Best Practices and Standards for Business Policy and Memo Formats

> **Source:** Exa Web Search | **Results:** 15 sources

## Essential Sections for Business Memos

### Standard Memo Format Components

1. **Header block**
   - To:
   - From:
   - Date:
   - Subject (RE:)

2. **Executive summary** — single paragraph summarizing the entire memo, placed after the header

3. **Background and context** — historical or technical information the reader needs (and nothing more)

4. **Key findings / analysis** — answers to questions directly, with evidence and supporting data

5. **Recommendations** — specific, feasible, cost-effective actions linked to findings

6. **Alternatives considered** — pros and cons of other options (why recommended is best)

7. **Implementation / next steps** — how and when to execute, any risks or obstacles

8. **Conclusion** — urgency, stakes if action is taken or not taken

Source: [Stanford Law Policy Memo Guidelines](https://law.stanford.edu/wp-content/uploads/2015/04/Policy-Memo-Guidelines-2.pdf), [Duke Writing Studio](https://twp.duke.edu/sites/twp.duke.edu/files/file-attachments/policy-memo.original.pdf)

## Policy Document Structure

### Core Policy Sections

1. **Title** — clear and specific (e.g., "Acceptable Use of Company Devices Policy")

2. **Document control**
   - Policy number/identifier
   - Version
   - Effective date
   - Review date
   - Owner
   - Approver

3. **Purpose** — why the policy exists (1-3 paragraphs)

4. **Scope** — who and what it applies to (employees, contractors, systems, locations)

5. **Policy statement** — the actual rule/position (heart of the policy)

6. **Definitions** — key terms that could be misunderstood

7. **Roles and responsibilities** — who does what (employees, managers, HR, IT, compliance)

8. **Procedures / requirements** — step-by-step rules (consider separating into SOP)

9. **Exceptions / exemptions** — how exceptions are approved and documented

10. **Enforcement / violations** — consequences, reporting, investigation process

11. **References / related documents** — laws, standards, forms, related policies

12. **Revision history** — version history of changes

Source: [PowerDMS](https://www.powerdms.com/policy-learning-center/best-practices-for-writing-corporate-policies-and-procedures), [Shopify UK](https://www.shopify.com/uk/blog/how-to-share-your-experience-in-memo-format)

## Memo Formatting Guidelines

### Block format style (standard business)

- Align each section with the left margin
- Separate blocks with line spacing
- Business font: Courier, Arial, or similar (12 point)

### Header example

```
To: All Employees
From: Director of Operations
Date: March 1, 2017
Subject: Unassigned Parking Spaces
```

Source: [smallbusiness.chron.com](https://smallbusiness.chron.com/write-new-company-policy-memo-10935.html)

### Writing principles

- **Inverted pyramid style**: most important information at the top, less significant details follow
- **Deductive paragraph structure**: main point in first sentence, supporting data follows
- **Brevity**: busy readers need concise content
- **Clarity**: write for "uninformed but intelligent" readers, not experts
- **Objectivity**: consider multiple perspectives, address strengths and weaknesses of all options

### Action items

- Use bullet points or bolded text to make actions stand out
- Include deadlines if applicable
- Make it obvious what the reader needs to do

Source: [Purdue OWL](https://owl.purdue.edu/owl/subject_specific_writing/professional_technical_writing/memos/format.html), [Harris School of Public Policy](https://writingworkshop.harris.uchicago.edu/2024/01/24/how-to-write-a-policy-memo-that-matters/)

## BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front) Format

A key approach used in government and policy memos:

1. **BLUF Statement** — explanation of the problem or issue at the start
   - States the problem in terms specific to the goal of the decision-maker
   - Tells the decision-maker why a policy change or research is needed
   - Briefly details the problem (focus on problem, not background)

2. **Pros and cons of policy areas** — leading up to recommendations
   - Review current policy — what it is and why it's done this way
   - Statement on necessity for change
   - Discuss alternatives to current policy option

3. **Recommendations** — which option is recommended and why
   - Identify which option will be recommended and which discounted
   - Lay out argument for why recommended option is better

4. **Implementation or Next Steps** — how and when to implement
   - If there are risks, costs, or obstacles, discuss them here
   - Timeline for implementation phases

5. **Conclusion** — return to the big picture
   - What happens if decision-maker does not implement?
   - What happens if they do?

Source: [Stanford Law](https://law.stanford.edu/wp-content/uploads/2015/04/Policy-Memo-Guidelines-2.pdf), [Harris School](https://harris.uchicago.edu/files/how_to_write_a_policy_memo_that_matters_0.pdf)

## Policy vs Memo vs SOP

| Document type | Purpose | Key question it answers |
|---|---|---|
| **Policy** | Formal rules and governance | What is required and why? |
| **Procedure / SOP** | Step-by-step instructions | How to do it? |
| **Memo** | Internal communication | What do they need to know or do now? |

## Writing Rules

### Language

- Use plain English, avoid jargon unless defined
- Use mandatory language carefully: must/shall = required; may/should/could = optional
- Avoid emotional or persuasive language in policy documents
- Define special terms on first use
- Use one term consistently for one concept

### Structure

- One idea per paragraph
- Use short paragraphs (3-4 sentences max)
- Use headings for navigation in long documents
- Use numbered sections for easy reference
- Keep policies modular

### Visual formatting

- Consistent fonts, spacing, and indentation
- Adequate white space
- Bold for section headings only
- Tables for responsibilities or approval matrices only
- Numbered sections and subsections consistently

## Document Control Elements

### For policies

- Document title
- Policy number or identifier
- Version and effective date
- Review date
- Owner and approver
- Supersedes/replaces notation
- Revision history table

### For memos

- To / From / Date / Subject
- Distribution list (if needed)
- Attachments list
- Reference documents
- Priority or deadline indicator

## Professional Standards Checklist

A complete business document should answer:

- What is this document?
- Why does it exist?
- Who does it apply to?
- What must be done?
- Who is responsible?
- When does it take effect?
- What happens if not followed?
- Where can reader find related guidance?
- Who approved it?
- When was it last updated?

---

## Key Writing Guidance

- **Policy = what and why; Procedure = how**
- Write in third person or institutional voice for policies
- Be clear and concise — do not overinclude details
- Separate policy from procedure and guidance
- Keep sentences short
- Use active voice
- Include approval workflow and periodic review process
