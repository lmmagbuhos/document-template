# Standards and Best Practices for Drafting Business Policies and Organizational Memos (Government/Military and Corporate)

> **Source:** Tavily Research Pro (autonomous deep research) | **Output:** Markdown (comprehensive report)

## BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front)
- For government/military memos, follow prescribed header elements, classification and portion-marking rules, and records/FOIA/retention requirements mandated in DoD and service correspondence manuals; use short BLUF openings and block style formats. For corporate memos, use the standard To/From/Date/Subject header, lead with a concise executive summary or BLUF when addressing executives, choose accessible, consistent styles (heading styles, readable fonts, 1" margins), and apply formal version control and naming conventions. Templates should be saved as reusable Word/Google Docs styles and exported to PDF for distribution; use e-signatures per ESIGN/eIDAS where allowed but follow agency rules for classified or record copies. [1], [2], [3], [4], [5], [12], [15], [17], [22]

## 2. Required header elements and common variants

### Core header fields and variants

- Standard memo header fields for both government and corporate memos are **To, From, Date, and Subject**; these are the baseline in Microsoft/Word templates and practitioner guidance for internal memos and policy notes. Corporate templates commonly present these at the top of the page under a "Memorandum" title. [17], [18], [36]

### Government/military specific header elements and placement

- Service correspondence manuals specify additional mandatory elements and precise placements: **Army AR 25-50** prescribes a six-element heading and places the date flush right on the same line as the office symbol after signature; it uses block style with heading, body, and closing parts and requires a record copy after signature. [3]
- Air Force and other service templates (e.g., **Tongue & Quill / AFH 33-337** guidance) provide required fields such as office title/letterhead, memo_for (recipient columns), memo_from, a required Subject (often Title Case), and explicit support for classification banners. AFH 33-337 gives guidance for headings, text, and closings. [2], [1]
- **Navy correspondence (SECNAV M-5216.5)** prescribes letterhead layout (e.g., "DEPARTMENT OF THE NAVY" placement, seal usage) for official stationery. [11]
- Military rules frequently require additional administrative fields such as office symbols, suspense dates (e.g., OPNAVINST practice for suspense dates), and memo numbers or control identifiers for administrative tracking. [10], [3]

### Classification/security markings and distribution fields

- Classified documents must show conspicuous overall classification banners and portion markings, and dissemination control markings where required; DoD marking guidance prescribes banner lines and portion markings and requires portion markings ahead of titles/subjects. Security Classification Guides (SCGs) have their own title, issuance and review dates, and identification of Original Classification Authority; interim classification memoranda may be used but must follow SCG table formats and be incorporated into SCGs within defined timelines. Classified material must be transmitted only over approved secure circuits. [12], [5], [4], [13]

### Contact info, CC/Distribution lists, memo numbers, routing/endorsements

- Corporate memos commonly include contact info and CC or distribution lists under the header; military memos include distribution lists or endorsement/routing blocks as prescribed by service manuals and may include suspense (S:) dates placed per AR 25-50 conventions. Unique memo identifiers, control numbers, or office symbols are standard in DoD/Army correspondence. [3], [17], [36]

## 3. Recommended body structure and section names

### High-level structure

- Government/military memoranda commonly use a **three-part block format**: Heading, Body, and Closing; policy memos emphasize short opening paragraphs that state the conclusion or action required up front (BLUF). Administrative memos may include references and enlist paragraph numbering per AFH guidance. [3], [1], [31], [32], [33]

### Recommended sections for clarity and decision use

Suggested canonical section set (applicable to both government and corporate policy memos, adaptable by audience):
- **Purpose/Subject** (one line after header)
- **BLUF / Executive summary** (see section 4 for BLUF conventions)
- **Background / Context**
- **Analysis / Discussion** (evidence, options, tradeoffs)
- **Recommendation(s)** (clear, prioritized)
- **Action items / Implementation steps** (who, what, when)
- **Attachments / Appendices / References** (supporting data)

Academic/policy writing guidance (Harvard, MIT, UChicago) emphasizes beginning with the problem and solution and allocating roughly equal space to problem definition and analysis/recommendations for policy memos. Corporate practices (Amazon six-pager, narrative memos) focus on disciplined narrative structure that forces rigorous thinking. [31], [32], [33], [29], [30]

### Paragraph and numbering conventions

- Where paragraph numbering is required (e.g., AFH automatic paragraph numbering in service templates), place tables between numbered paragraphs and do not count them toward numbering. Block style single-spaced paragraphs with an extra space between paragraphs is common in memos; many Word templates use run-in headings and avoid indenting. [2], [1], [19], [34], [45]

## 4. BLUF specifics (placement, length, tone, bullets vs paragraphs, multiple BLUFs)

### Placement and purpose

- Leading with BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front) is standard in military and policy circles and is recommended for executive audiences and policy memos; BLUF typically appears at the very start of the body or as the first paragraph following the subject. [32], [33], [31]

### Length and tone

- BLUF should be short and direct—typically **one to three sentences** for routine memos; for executive memos the "executive summary" can be slightly longer but still concise. Practitioner guidance for internal memos suggests introductions of one to three sentences summarizing purpose and intent. [17], [16]

### Bullets vs paragraph narrative

- Use bullets to list discrete action items, decisions, and timelines for clarity in manager and staff communications. Narrative memos used in some corporate cultures (e.g., **Amazon six-pager**) prefer tightly argued paragraphs and discourage overuse of bullets in the main analytic narrative; appendices can contain tables and bulleted lists. Choose format to match audience expectations: executives often prefer short BLUF + bullets for actions, while deep review audiences may expect coherent narrative sections. [29], [30], [17], [35]

### Multiple BLUF levels

- When addressing layered audiences (e.g., senior leaders who need a single sentence decision and staff who need details), consider a **two-tier BLUF**: a one-sentence top-line BLUF for executives, immediately followed by a 1–2 paragraph expanded summary for deputies/managers before background and analysis. Policy memo guides recommend placing the most important recommendations first and then expanding. [32], [33], [31]

## 5. Visual and typographic formatting (fonts, sizes, margins, spacing, heading hierarchy)

### Readable, accessible defaults

Common, sanctioned defaults in templates and style guidance include:
- **Margins:** 1-inch (normal/printable) layouts are standard in Word templates and video walkthroughs. [19]
- **Fonts:** Times New Roman 12 pt is a default in some Air Force templates and older style guides; corporate guidance often accepts modern sans-serifs for screen readability (e.g., Arial/Calibri 11); APA/Chicago guidance lists acceptable readable fonts (e.g., Times New Roman 12, Arial 11, Calibri 11). Choose one readable font and apply consistently. [2], [1], [37], [38]

### Heading hierarchy and styles

- Use built-in heading styles (Heading 1…Heading 4) rather than manual formatting to maintain consistent hierarchy and to support navigation and accessibility; limit heading levels to what the document size requires (typically up to four for long documents). Sentence-style capitalization for headings is recommended in the Microsoft style guide; headings should be specific and brief. [16], [23], [16]

### Spacing, weights, bullets, and numbering

- Block paragraphs, single spacing within paragraphs, and a blank line between paragraphs are common memo conventions; bullet lists are appropriate for discrete items and action lists. Use consistent bullet and numbering styles and avoid manual spacing that breaks style consistency. Apply character styles to emphasize small bits (bold for headings) rather than ad-hoc formatting. [19], [16], [34], [35]

### Tables, figures, printable vs screen layouts, templates

- Tables and figures are permissible; in military templates tables typically do not count toward paragraph numbering. For distribution: prepare a printable, print-optimized layout (1-inch margins, readable serif or sans-serif fonts) and a screen-optimized version (larger headings, hyperlinks, accessible alt text), then export to PDF for noneditable distribution. Save templates as reusable Word/Google Doc styles to ensure consistency. Word and Google offer memo templates and style features to enforce these settings. [2], [18], [20], [22], [19], [12], [34]

## 6. Accessibility, document metadata, version control, file naming, retention, distribution, signatures, classification/FOIA considerations

### Accessibility and alt text

- Provide alt text for all informative graphics and an empty alt attribute for purely decorative images; apply heading styles for screen-reader navigation and avoid inserting extra blank lines (screen readers will announce them). Technical style guides and corporate style docs recommend short descriptive alt text for icons and graphics. [28], [39], [16]

### Document metadata and PDF export

- Use document metadata (title, author, keywords) consistently when creating templates and before PDF export; this aids records management and discovery. Save or export final, approved versions as PDF for distribution and record keeping where appropriate. [18], [20], [15]

### Version control and file naming

- Adopt a clear versioning convention (major.minor: e.g., v1.0 for approved final, v0.1 for drafts), include date in YYYYMMDD or ISO formats for filenames, and maintain a revision history log for important documents. Centralized Document Management Systems are recommended for tracking versions and restricting access to current approved versions. [24], [41], [40], [25], [26]

### Retention and records management (DoD emphasis)

- **DoD Instruction 5015.02** remains the governing DoD records policy; newer manuals/tools (DoD Manual 8180.01 and DTM-22-001) provide criteria for electronic records management tools, require safe-harbor periods for deleted IT data, require disposition support, and stress interoperability of records across IT systems. Create and maintain record copies per service directives (for example, AR 25-50 requires one record copy after signing). FOIA and component FOIA program responsibilities (training, requester service centers) also intersect with records management. [15], [3], [7], [8], [14]

### Distribution, FOIA, and classified material

- Follow classification marking guidance for banners and portion markings; classification decisions must identify OCA and determine duration/declassification instructions; some guides require "Declassify on" or event-based declassification lines. FOIA programs require agencies to track performance and appoint FOIA officers; FOIA exemptions and guidance are maintained by DOJ and agencies. Classified material distribution is limited to approved secure circuits and must follow marking and handling rules. Protected unclassified material (CUI) and SCGs have specific release rules. [4], [5], [6], [8], [9], [13]

### Signatures: wet, scanned, e-signature

- When legally acceptable, modern e-signature tools are supported by **ESIGN in the U.S.** and **eIDAS for EU** (Google Workspace eSignature notes compliance); organizations must still follow internal rules for recordkeeping and any prohibitions for classified or otherwise restricted documents. For printed/military forms, AR 25-50 allows signatures in blue or black ink and requires a record copy after signing. For classified materials, organizational policy will control whether wet signatures are required. [22], [3], [15], [13]

## 7. Professional readiness checklist for authors and approvers

Each line is a pass/fail item to confirm before release:

- **Header:** To/From/Date/Subject present and in correct format for the audience; office symbol/memo number added if required. [17], [3]
- **Classification:** Overall banner and portion markings present and accurate; "Declassify on" or event line included if required; transmission method approved. [12], [5], [4]
- **BLUF/Executive summary:** Present, one-line top-line for execs plus expanded summary for deputies if needed. [32], [33]
- **Structure:** Background, analysis, recommendations, and actions included and clearly labeled. [31], [35]
- **Formatting:** Use approved template or saved styles; heading styles applied; fonts and margins set to organizational standard. [18], [16], [19]
- **Accessibility:** Headings used, alt text for images, no extra blank lines, document properties filled. [39], [28]
- **Version control:** Filename uses agreed convention (date YYYYMMDD + v#); revision log attached or maintained. [24], [41]
- **Records/FOIA compliance:** One record copy retained per policy; FOIA and retention metadata present if required. [15], [7], [8]
- **Signatures:** Signature method (wet/scanned/e-signature) authorized for document type and classification; signature block correct. [22], [3]
- **Approvals:** Routing/endorsements captured and approvals documented; suspense/completion dates included where required. [10], [3]
- **Finalization:** Proofread, verified facts, converted to PDF for distribution if noneditable, mailing/list of recipients confirmed. [18], [36]

## 8. Three concise annotated sample templates (plain text; Word/Google Docs-friendly)

Each template uses the common To/From/Date/Subject header and a BLUF. Presented in plain text that can be copy/pasted into Word or Google Docs and saved as a template or style set.

### 1) Executive-level memo (one-page, BLUF + actions)

```
Memorandum
To: [Senior Leader / Title]
From: [Author / Title / Office Symbol]
Date: [YYYY-MM-DD]
Subject: [Concise Title — Title Case]

BLUF: [One sentence — primary decision or recommendation.]

Summary: [One short paragraph expanding BLUF — 2–4 sentences for context, high-level risk/benefit.]

Recommended action:
- [Primary action — who does what, by when.]
- [Secondary action(s) if any.]

Next steps / Approval needed: [List approvals or endorsements required; concise deadlines.]

Contact: [Name, phone/email]

(Attach: [Key supporting appendix or dataset])
```

### 2) Manager-level memo (two pages; BLUF + analysis + actions)

```
Memorandum
To: [Team/Manager(s)]
From: [Author / Title / Division]
Date: [YYYY-MM-DD]
Subject: [Descriptive Subject Line — Title Case]

BLUF: [1–2 sentences — decision/recommendation and required action.]

Background: [2–4 short paragraphs — why memo is needed; reference prior actions or directives.]

Analysis: [Bulleted or short-paragraph presentation of options, pros/cons, constraints (3–6 bullets or short paras).]

Recommendation & Actions:
1. Recommendation: [Clear statement.]
2. Actions:
   - [Action 1 — Responsible — Date]
   - [Action 2 — Responsible — Date]

Appendix: [Data tables, timeline, references]
```

### 3) Staff-level memo / Procedure note (operational)

```
Memorandum
To: [Staff / Distribution List]
From: [Author / Title]
Date: [YYYY-MM-DD]
Subject: [Procedure or Task — Title Case]

Purpose/BLUF: [One sentence: what to do, who must act.]

Steps / Implementation:
- Step 1 — [Task] — [Responsible] — [Due date]
- Step 2 — [Task] — [Responsible] — [Due date]

References: [Policy or SOP citations; attach template forms.]

Records: [Where to file the record copy / DMS location]
```

## 9. Comparison: U.S. federal/DoD/military guidance vs common corporate style guides

### Core differences (mandate vs preference)

**Military/DoD:** Correspondence and records behavior is prescriptive — specific header elements, paragraph numbering, classification marking, office symbols, and mandated retention and FOIA organizational obligations. Official manuals (AR 25-50, AFH 33-337, SECNAV M-5216.5) set required formats and handling rules; DoD records rules (DoD Instruction 5015.02, Manual 8180.01, DTM-22-001) define records management and IT system obligations. **Compliance is mandatory.** [3], [1], [11], [15], [4]

**Corporate:** Style guides and templates are typically advisory and focus on clarity, brand consistency, and usability (e.g., Microsoft/Word templates, Apple/IBM style guidance, corporate templates). Corporate narrative cultures (Amazon six-pager) may intentionally avoid bullets and demand long narrative analysis; others prefer short memos with bullets. Corporate guidance emphasizes templates, reusable styles, accessible headings, and brand identity. [17], [18], [27], [28], [29], [30]

### Overlap and pragmatic synthesis

- Both domains require clarity, a clear top-line message, controlled distribution, version control, and retention of final approved versions; both recommend reusable templates and applying heading styles for accessibility.
- Use the stricter set of rules when a memo crosses into the regulated domain (e.g., government contractors should follow agency requirements for records and classification when applicable). [15], [22], [16], [24]

### Authoritative sources and prioritization

- Prioritize authoritative issuances for regulated documents: DoD Instructions/Manuals and service correspondence manuals for military/federal work; DOJ/OPM/agency FOIA guidance for public-release obligations.
- For corporate style and formatting defaults, prioritize the organization's corporate style guide, then major vendors' guidance (Microsoft, Google) for template behavior and e-signatures. [3], [4], [5], [7], [8], [17], [22], [18], [20]

## 10. Evidence gaps (what the reviewed sources do not specify)

- **No single universal font mandate:** No single source provides a universal, cross-sector exact font + size mandate for all memo types; guidance varies by organization (Times New Roman 12pt appears in certain templates, while corporate guides allow Arial/Calibri). The evidence supports recommending consistent, readable fonts but not a single mandatory font across sectors. [2], [37], [38]
- **Signature policy ambiguity:** There is no authoritative government-wide rule that specifies when wet signatures must be used vs. scanned/e-signatures for all document types and classification levels; e-signature legality is discussed (ESIGN/eIDAS compliance) but organizational policy and classification handling determine acceptability. [22], [3], [13]
- **No universal template file:** The sources do not provide a single, standard Word/Google Docs file template to attach; practitioner templates and vendor templates are available (Microsoft/Google/third-party) and should be customized to agency/corporate rules. [18], [20], [36]

## 11. Short recommended implementation checklist

- Adopt or adapt an organizational memo template that implements required header elements (To/From/Date/Subject) and service-specific fields where applicable; store as a controlled template in the org's DMS. [18], [3], [2]
- Configure Word/Google Docs styles (Heading 1–4, Normal, List) and save defaults for the org; provide one-page executive and two-page manager templates. [23], [16], [18]
- Define filename and versioning policy (YYYYMMDD_v#_author initials) and enforce via DMS; maintain revision log for major policy docs. [24], [26], [41]
- Apply accessibility checks (headings, alt text, no extra blank lines) before finalizing and exporting to PDF; document metadata should be filled. [39], [28], [20]
- For classified or record documents, follow service/agency marking, routing, retention, and transmission rules; ensure records manager signs off on disposition schedule. [12], [15], [7]
- Specify signature policy (wet vs e-signature) in the org's correspondence policy consistent with legal and classification constraints; where lawful, use ESIGN-compliant e-signature providers. [22], [3]

---

## 12. Conclusion

Apply the mandatory, prescriptive rules in DoD/service correspondence and records guidance where applicable; for corporate communications, apply concise BLUF practices, consistent styles (heading styles, readable fonts, 1-inch margins), and strong version control/retention. Save approved templates as Word/Google styles, use e-signatures in compliance with legal and agency rules, and always validate classification and FOIA/records implications before release.
